Project Overview

ResPredAI is an open-source machine learning pipeline designed to predict antimicrobial resistance (AMR) in Gram-negative bloodstream infections. Built to support clinical decision-making, it implements the methodology described in our research published in npj Digital Medicine.

The tool provides a comprehensive workflow for training, validating, and deploying predictive models that can help clinicians anticipate bacterial resistance patterns, enabling more informed antibiotic prescribing decisions.

Multiple ML Models

Support for 8 algorithms including Random Forest, XGBoost, CatBoost, and TabPFN

Rigorous Validation

Nested cross-validation with group-aware stratification to prevent data leakage

Feature Importance

Built-in feature importance extraction with SHAP-based explanations

Comprehensive Docs

Full documentation with tutorials, CLI reference, and examples

Original Research

npj Digital Medicine

Artificial Intelligence model to predict resistances in Gram-negative bloodstream infections

Bonazzetti, C., Rocchi, E., Toschi, A. et al.

npj Digital Medicine 8, 319 (2025)

Abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) models are promising tools for predicting antimicrobial susceptibility in gram-negative bloodstream infections (GN-BSI). Single-center study on hospitalized patients with GN-BSI, over 7-year period, aimed to predict resistance to fluoroquinolones (FQ-R), third generation cephalosporins (3GC-R), beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitors (BL/BLI-R) and carbapenems (C-R) was performed. Analyses were carried out within a machine learning framework, developed using the scikit-learn Python package. Overall, 2552 patients were included. Enterobacterales accounted for 85.5% of isolates, with E. coli, Klebsiella spp, and Proteus spp being most common. Distribution of resistance was FQ-R 48.6%, 3GC-R 40.1%, BL/BLI-R 29.9%, and C-R 16.9%. Models' validation showed good performance predicting antibiotic resistance for all four resistance classes, with the best performance for C-R (AUC-ROC 0.921 Â± 0.013). The developed pipeline has been made available (https://github.com/EttoreRocchi/ResPredAI), along with documentation for running the same workflow on a different dataset, to account for local epidemiology and clinical features.
